Scalable RL-Based Toolpath Planner for 3D Printing

Paper arXiv Code (coming soon)

 

This paper introduces a reinforcement learning-based planner that optimizes 3D printing toolpaths for large-scale applications, demonstrating generality and efficiency across different printing methods with significant improvements in model quality and reduction of printing errors.

Abstract

We presents an efficient reinforcement learning (RL) based planner for computing optimized 3D printing toolpaths, which can work on graphs on large scales by constructing the state space on-the-fly. The planner can cover different 3D printing applications by defining their corresponding reward functions and state spaces. Toolpath generation problems in wire-frame printing, continuous fiber printing, and metallic printing are selected here to demonstrate generality. The resultant toolpaths have been applied in physical experiments to verify the performance of the planner. By this planner, wire-frame models with up to 3.3k struts can be successfully printed, up to 93.3% of sharp turns on continuous fiber toolpaths can be avoided, and the thermal distortion in metallic printing can be reduced by 24.9%.

How our system works

 

Our RL-based planner is used to compute optimized toolpaths for 3D printing by navigating a graph. The method involves exploring a graph structured into local sub-graphs (LSGs) with defined neighbor rings, focusing on a current node. The planner uses a deep Q-network to evaluate and select the next best node based on learned Q-values, considering the current state of exploration, which incorporates the positions of nodes and their adjacency relationships. This process highlights changes and decisions in the path planning through color-coded blocks to visualize differences in adjacency matrices, aiding in understanding the transition between states. The method iteratively applies this process to navigate through the graph, ultimately determining an optimized toolpath, demonstrated through a series of illustrative steps and the resulting toolpath. This approach showcases how reinforcement learning can be effectively applied to complex pathfinding problems in 3D printing applications.


Overview

 

This method utilizes an advanced planner for optimizing 3D printing toolpaths across three applications: wire-frame models, continuous fiber-reinforced layers, and LPBF metal printing. Starting with specific input graphs for each task, the planner generates tailored toolpaths that meet manufacturing objectives. These paths are visualized in distinct colors—red, green, and blue arrows—to indicate different sections or movements. The effectiveness of these optimized toolpaths is confirmed through physical experiments, demonstrating the planner's capability to enhance efficiency and quality in diverse 3D printing processes.


Experimental results

Progressive results of the planned toolpaths

Progressive results of the planned toolpaths for (a) wire-frame printing, (b) CCF printing, and (c) metallic printing. For CCF printing, bold lines are those edges traveled twice. The first graph of each printing process is the input graph


Comparisons of CCF printing for tensile test

Comparisons of CCF printing for the Shell model: (top) the toolpath generated by the dual-graph based DFS method and (bottom) our toolpath. As the number of sharp turns have been significantly reduced on our toolpath, stronger mechanical strength is observed in the tensile test. Specifically, specimen fabricated by our toolpath shows 29% increased breaking force while using 28% less CCF.


Comparisons of wireframe printing

Comparison of the printing results of two models by using the toolpath generated by peeling heuristic vs. the toolpath generated by our RL-based planner. When using the peeling-like toolpath, we can observe the failure cases of large bending deformation on struts, i.e., with large displacements caused by gravity. Also, stringing phenomenon occurs due to frequent lifting of the tool


Comparisons of metallic printing for thermal warpage study

Thermal warpage study using different toolpaths to print the Femur model on the initial plate, including the zigzag toolpath, the chessboard toolpath and our toolpath. Distortions are measured by 3D scanner and displayed as colormaps. Distortion on the initial plate is 0.10mm. Maximum distortion on the specimen printed by our toolpath is 1.84mm, which is reduced by 24.90% and 24.28% compared to the zigzag toolpath (2.45mm) and the chessboard toolpath (2.43mm).
